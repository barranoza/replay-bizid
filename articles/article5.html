Hydration: How Much Water Do You Really Need?

By Julia Martinez, RN • March 22, 2024 • 3 Min Read

We've all heard the "eight glasses a day" rule. But is it actually rooted in science? Water is essential to every single bodily function, yet many people walk around in a state of mild, chronic dehydration.

The Real Formula for Hydration

The U.S. National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine determined that an adequate daily fluid intake is about 15.5 cups (3.7 liters) for men and 11.5 cups (2.7 liters) for women. However, this includes water from all beverages and foods.

Did you know? About 20% of your daily fluid intake actually comes from food! Fruits like watermelon and spinach are almost entirely water.

Signs of Dehydration

Thirst is actually a late sign of dehydration. If you are thirsty, you are already slightly dehydrated. Look out for these signs:

  • Dark-colored urine
  • Unexplained afternoon fatigue
  • Frequent mild headaches
  • Dry skin and lips

How to Drink More Water

Keep a reusable bottle on your desk. Flavor your water with a slice of lemon or cucumber if plain water bores you. Make it a habit to drink a full glass right when you wake up to rehydrate after an 8-hour sleep.
